Genres

Romance

According to the Romance Writers of America, "Two basic elements comprise every romance novel: a central love story and an emotionally-satisfying and optimistic ending." Both the conflict and the climax of the novel should be directly related to that core theme of developing a romantic relationship, although the novel can also contain subplots that do not specifically relate to the main characters' romantic love. Other definitions of a romance novel may be broader, including other plots and endings or more than two people, or narrower, restricting the types of romances or conflicts.

Biography

Biography, as interpreted by various authors, is the detailed account of an individual's life, combining factual accuracy with narrative artistry. According to James Boswell, one of the pioneers of modern biography, it is a way to preserve a person’s character and legacy through storytelling. Leon Edel emphasizes biography as a form of literary art that reconstructs a life with insight and empathy while balancing truth and interpretation. Nigel Hamilton views it as both a historical document and a compelling narrative that explores the motivations and experiences shaping a person’s life. Collectively, these perspectives highlight biography as a blend of historical accuracy, personal insight, and storytelling aimed at understanding and commemorating lives.

Comics

Comics, as described by authors like Scott McCloud and Will Eisner, are a unique art form combining sequential images and text to tell stories or convey ideas. Defined as "sequential art," they rely on the interplay between visuals and words to create meaning, with a focus on rhythm, pacing, and narrative depth. Authors such as Neil Gaiman and Alan Moore highlight the medium's potential to explore complex themes and emotions, while Art Spiegelman and others emphasize its legitimacy as a serious storytelling medium capable of cultural and artistic significance. Comics stand out as a versatile fusion of literature and visual art.

Fiction

Fiction is the telling of stories which are not real. More specifically, fiction is an imaginative form of narrative, one of the four basic rhetorical modes. Although the word fiction is derived from the Latin fingo, fingere, finxi, fictum, "to form, create", works of fiction need not be entirely imaginary and may include real people, places, and events. Fiction may be either written or oral. Although not all fiction is necessarily artistic, fiction is largely perceived as a form of art or entertainment. The ability to create fiction and other artistic works is considered to be a fundamental aspect of human culture, one of the defining characteristics of humanity.

Mystery

The mystery genre is a genre of fiction that follows a crime (like a murder or a disappearance) from the moment it is committed to the moment it is solved. Mystery novels are often called “whodunnits” because they turn the reader into a detective trying to figure out the who, what, when, and how of a particular crime. Most mysteries feature a detective or private eye solving a case as the central character.
